      • Common Reading ProgramsThe practice of assigning students “common reading”—asking them to read the same book simultaneously—has gained popularity in recent years as secondary schools, colleges, and universities have sought new ways to enrich the campus experience. Like similar public reading initiatives sponsored by cities, libraries, and television shows, educational common reading programs rest on a simple idea: that reading the same book brings people closer together as a community by creating common ground for discussion. This I Believe books provide rich opportunities for students engaged in a common reading program. Reading a collection of This I Believe essays: • encourages students to read beyond textbooks. • enriches the campus community through exploration of personal values and beliefs. • raises awareness and tolerance of intergenerational and cultural likenesses and differences. • promotes civic discourse and critical thinking. • increases student-to-student interaction. • integrates an academic and social experience into the campus community. • appeals to both males and females. • has potential for strong programming opportunities beyond the reading experience, including writing, listening, and podcasting of personal essays.       • About UsThis I Believe, Inc., was founded in 2004 as a not-for-profit organization that engages youth and adults from all walks of life in writing, sharing, and discussing brief essays about the core values that guide their daily lives. This I Believe is based on a 1950s radio program of the same name, hosted by acclaimed journalist Edward R. Murrow. Each day, Americans gathered by their radios to hear compelling essays from the likes of Eleanor Roosevelt, Jackie Robinson, Helen Keller, and Harry Truman as well as corporate leaders, cab drivers, scientists, and secretaries—anyone able to distill into a few minutes the guiding principles by which they lived. These essayists’ words brought comfort and inspiration to a country worried about the Cold War, McCarthyism, and racial division. (These essays are now featured in weekly broadcasts on Bob Edwards‘ satellite and public radio shows.) In reviving This I Believe, executive producer Dan Gediman says, “The goal is not to persuade Americans to agree on the same beliefs. Rather, the hope is to encourage people to begin the much more difficult task of developing respect for beliefs different from their own.” Selected contemporary This I Believe essays were featured in regular broadcasts on…

      • FAQIs there a deadline to submit an essay? No. We welcome your essay at any time. How long should my essay be? Between 350 and 500 words, or about three minutes when read aloud at your natural speaking pace. How can I improve my essay before I submit it? First, make sure your essay is framed in a concrete belief or conviction (we receive many wonderful essays that contain no statement of belief). Then, tell us a compelling story about how you came to hold that belief, or a time that belief was challenged, or how that belief shapes your daily activities. Try sharing one belief instead of composing a list of all of your beliefs. How do I know you received my essay? When you successfully submit your essay through our website, you will see an on-screen message that your essay was received. In addition, you should receive a confirmation email. Can I submit an essay via regular mail? We are no longer accepting essays by U.S. Mail. To have your essay included in our online Essay Collection, please submit your essay on-line.     Beyond the Tags

    Jason Chen - Santa Cruz, California

    Jason Chen describes his journey of moving to the US at the age of 13 and attending a Christian school, despite initially holding strong opinions against people of religion. Through interactions with his teachers and classmates, Jason challenges his preconceived notions and learns the importance of understanding people's unique experiences. He emphasizes the need to go beyond superficial differences and connect with others on a deeper, more human level.

    I was at the age of 13 when I came to the US. Learning that “Village Christian School” was the school that I was going to attend, my attention was immediately drawn towards the one word I don’t know - Christian.

    Strong rejection would be a lenient way to describe my attitude toward Christianity at the time. Growing up in China, I was exposed to a relatively homogenous culture and a very narrow perspective on religion. Moving into 7th grade I was heavily against the concept of religion and held strong opinions against people of religion. I believed anyone with decent logical thinking abilities would immediately notice its flaws. Furthermore, I felt that those who embraced religion must be mentally weak and in need of some form of emotional support. I mean, what else would be the purpose of religion right?

    There was not a sudden realization that made me notice I held prejudice against Christians. Interacting with teachers and classmates around me, however, allowed me to slowly recognize my misconceptions.

    As I interacted with these individuals, I started to question my earlier assumptions about religion. This journey taught me firsthand the importance of challenging my own prejudices and assumptions. I had hastily labeled people based on their religious beliefs, without truly understanding who they were as individuals. The realization has made me aware of how easily we can fall into the trap of judging others based on superficial differences, whether it be religion, race, political views, or any other distinctions that seem to set us apart. In a world where our online experiences often reinforce our existing beliefs and biases, I've learned to consciously make an effort to see beyond these tags and connect with others on a deeper, more human level.

    Starting from that point, I begin to realize many other instances where I preemptively put tags among people that I don’t have a great understanding of. People are the way they are for a reason. And “because they are all dumb!” is probably not the correct answer. Whenever I make assumptions about someone, I’ve learned that it is best to ask myself “Why?” By challenging my preconceived notions and making an effort to understand people's unique experiences, I have been able to broaden my perspective and foster genuine connections with others from diverse backgrounds.

    I am still not a Christian, but at least I am not judging them by our differences.

    Jason Chen wrote this essay for his writing class at University of California, Santa Cruz. He is currenty a second-student majored in Comptuter Science.
